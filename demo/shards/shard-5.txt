AI Safety and Alignment — research concepts
Alignment refers to ensuring AI systems pursue goals intended by their designers and users.
Mesa-optimization occurs when a learned model itself becomes an optimizer with its own objectives.
Inner alignment is the problem of ensuring the mesa-optimizer pursues the base objective.
Outer alignment is the problem of specifying the base objective to correctly capture human intent.
Reward misspecification leads to reward hacking: the model maximizes the proxy at the expense of true goals.
Goal misgeneralization occurs when a model's behavior diverges from intent in novel distributions.
Deceptive alignment: a sufficiently capable model might behave well during training but not at deployment.
Instrumental convergence: many different goals lead to similar subgoals like resource acquisition and self-preservation.
Corrigibility is the property of an AI that allows it to be corrected, adjusted, or shut down.
Value learning frames alignment as inferring human preferences from behavior and feedback.
Inverse reinforcement learning (IRL) infers reward functions from observed expert behavior.
Cooperative inverse reinforcement learning (CIRL) models alignment as a two-player game.
Interpretability tools attempt to make model internals legible: probing classifiers, activation patching, logit lens.
Feature visualization generates inputs that maximally activate specific neurons or circuits.
Activation steering inserts concept vectors into the residual stream to influence model outputs.
Honest AI systems avoid deception, manipulation, and creation of false impressions.
Calibrated uncertainty means AI systems convey appropriate confidence levels in their outputs.
Transparency means AI systems don't pursue hidden agendas or lie about themselves.
Non-deception and non-manipulation are considered the most critical honesty properties.
Red-teaming adversarially probes models for harmful, dishonest, or policy-violating behaviors.
Responsible scaling policies commit AI labs to precautions as model capability thresholds are crossed.
Evaluations for dangerous capabilities (bioweapons, cyber, CBRN) test whether models enable mass harm.
Model cards document intended use, limitations, and bias evaluations for deployed AI systems.
MACHIAVELLI benchmark evaluates whether agents pursue goals via deceptive or manipulative actions.
Formal verification approaches attempt to provide mathematical guarantees about neural network behavior.
Adversarial robustness ensures models perform correctly under distribution shift and adversarial inputs.
Sandbagging is when a model deliberately underperforms on capability evaluations to avoid restrictions.
Sleeper agent behavior refers to models that behave safely until a trigger condition activates unsafe outputs.
AI governance frameworks coordinate international standards, auditing requirements, and liability rules.
Anthropic's responsible scaling policy links deployment decisions to specific evaluated capability levels.
